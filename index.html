Theory: Protactinium-234 Half-Life Experiment

The Decay Chain

This experiment uses the uranium-238 decay chain:

U-238 → Th-234 → Pa-234 → U-234
  • Uranium-238: Extremely long half-life (4.5 billion years), provides constant supply
  • Thorium-234: Half-life of 24.1 days, stays in aqueous layer, emits weak beta particles
  • Protactinium-234: Half-life of ~70 seconds, dissolves in organic layer, emits energetic beta particles (2.32 MeV)

Chemical Separation

The bottle contains two layers:

  • Aqueous layer (bottom, yellow): Uranyl nitrate solution with U-238 and Th-234
  • Organic layer (top, darker yellow): Iso-butyl methyl ketone or amyl acetate

When shaken, uranium and protactinium form chloride complexes that dissolve in the organic layer (~95% extraction), while thorium remains in the aqueous layer.

Detection

The Geiger-Müller tube is positioned near the top of the bottle to detect:

  • High-energy beta particles from Pa-234 decay (primarily)
  • Some background radiation
  • NOT the alpha particles from U-238 (low energy, absorbed)
  • NOT the weak beta particles from Th-234 (mostly absorbed by position)

Exponential Decay

After separation, Pa-234 in the organic layer decays exponentially:

Activity A(t) = A₀ × e^(-λt)
where λ = ln(2)/T₁/₂ = 0.0099 s⁻¹
Initial activity A₀ ≈ 50 Bq after shaking

The count rate decreases with the same exponential pattern, allowing determination of the half-life. The higher initial activity (~50 Bq) provides better counting statistics and reduces relative uncertainty in measurements.

Experimental Procedure

  1. Shake the bottle vigorously for 15 seconds
  2. Wait for layers to separate (a few seconds)
  3. Start the timer and begin counting
  4. Record total counts every 10 seconds
  5. Continue for at least 3 minutes (180 seconds)
  6. Measure background radiation separately
  7. Correct counts for background
  8. Calculate count rate = (counts in interval) / (time interval)
  9. Plot ln(count rate) vs time to get straight line
  10. Determine half-life from the graph

Data Analysis

Students should:

  • Record the total counts shown every 10 seconds
  • Note: The experimental counts include both Pa-234 decay AND background radiation (~0.5 Bq or ~5 counts per 10 seconds)
  • Subtract background counts (from background measurement)
  • Calculate count rate for each 10-second interval
  • Plot count rate vs time to see exponential decay
  • Plot ln(count rate) vs time to get straight line with slope = -λ
  • Calculate half-life: T₁/₂ = ln(2)/λ ≈ 70 seconds

Sources of Uncertainty

  • Statistical variation (Poisson distribution) in radioactive decay
  • Incomplete extraction of Pa-234 into organic layer
  • Some detection of Th-234 beta particles from aqueous layer
  • Background radiation fluctuations
  • Timing precision when starting/stopping measurements
